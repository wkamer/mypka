# ICOR - PKM like a Pro

# Layer 1: Concepts

## 01. What is PKM and a PKM System?

### Section 1 of 13

PKM is a process that turns information into action aligned with your goals, not just a note-taking app.

PKM sits in the personal information quadrant of the ICOR Framework alongside BKM, PPM, and BPM.
The Knowledge Management Cycle has five stages: Discover, Capture, Process, Action, Share.
Note-taking is only one step (Capture). PKM covers the full lifecycle of information.
Define your processes first, then pick tools that support them. Tools are replaceable, processes are not.
"We shape our tools, and thereafter our tools shape us." Marshall McLuhan

You have a folder full of meeting notes. You have a bookmarking app with 400 saved articles. You have voice memos on your phone, highlights in your Kindle, and screenshots scattered across three devices. All of this is information you captured at some point because it felt important. But right now, if someone asked you to find the one insight that changed how you approached your Q3 strategy, you would spend twenty minutes looking and probably give up.

That is the gap between collecting information and managing knowledge. Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) closes that gap. It is not another app. It is not a filing system. It is a process that turns the information flowing through your life into actions that move you toward your goals.

This lesson defines PKM using the ICOR methodology and gives you the foundation for every concept, workflow, and implementation lesson that follows in this course.

### Section 2 of 13

The ICOR Definition of PKM

Let's set a clear definition and stick to it throughout the course. Personal Knowledge Management is a process involving the discovery, capture, and processing of information that leads to the necessary actions to help you achieve your goals.

Three words carry the weight of that definition.

Information: the raw material. Articles, conversations, data points, ideas, observations, and anything else that enters your world from the outside or originates inside your own thinking. As you saw in Digital Note-Taking like a Pro, notes can carry both information and action content. You will learn how both sources of information work in Inner World and Outer World later in this course.

Action: the output. Tasks, decisions, projects, experiments, conversations, and deliverables that move your work forward. Information without action is a library nobody visits.

Goals: the filter. Your professional and personal objectives determine which information deserves attention and which actions are worth taking. Without goals, you capture everything and act on nothing.

These three elements form a straight line: Information feeds Action, and Action drives you toward Goals. But PKM is not just about the endpoints. It also involves the intermediate processes of structuring and linking ideas until you truly grasp a concept or topic. Remember how How to link notes? showed you that connecting notes creates compound understanding? PKM builds on that same principle at a system level. Only through that deep understanding can you create the specific actions you actually need.

> Tip: When you evaluate any piece of information, ask three questions in order. "Does this connect to my goals?" Then, "What action could this lead to?" Then, "Where should I store this so I can find it when the action is due?" If you cannot answer the first question, the information probably does not belong in your system.

## 02. Why Do You Need a PKM System?

### Section 1 of 9

You need a PKM system for four reasons: capturing ideas, compounding knowledge, enabling reflection, and converting information into action.

Captured ideas are the starting point of all meaningful work. Without capture, insights disappear.
Every new note connected to existing notes creates compound knowledge growth over time.
Reflection turns raw experience into patterns, and patterns sharpen your decisions.
Information that never becomes a task, decision, or deliverable is just organized hoarding.
"Every intellectual endeavour starts with a note." Sonke Ahrens, How to Take Smart Notes

You read an article last month about a new market opportunity. You had a reaction to it. Today, in a strategy meeting, someone raises the exact same opportunity. You know you read about it. But you cannot remember where, what the data said, or what you thought. So you sit there, silent, while a colleague who wrote it down speaks with authority.

This happens every day in every industry. Not because professionals lack intelligence. Because they lack a system. The information was there. The moment of relevance arrived. But without a structured way to capture, connect, and retrieve knowledge, the moment passed. This is the gap a PKM system closes.

In Digital Note-Taking like a Pro, you learned what a note is, how to store notes properly, and how to link notes together. Those skills gave you the mechanics. This lesson explains why those mechanics matter at a larger scale. It presents four reasons you need a Personal Knowledge Management system. Each reason builds on the last, and together they form the case for why this course exists.

## 03. Shallow Thinking vs. Deep Thinking

### Section 1 of 10

Your PKM needs two thinking modes: Shallow Thinking for fast capture and tagging, Deep Thinking for connecting ideas and building understanding.

Shallow Thinking handles volume: tag, classify, file. Speed is the goal, not comprehension.
Deep Thinking handles meaning: connect ideas, write synthesis, explore on a visual canvas.
Not every note needs deep processing. Most stay shallow. Promote selectively when a question forms.
Separate the two modes in your schedule. Mixing them slows both down.
"You have to work hard to get your thinking clean to make it simple. But it's worth it in the end, because once you get there, you can move mountains.", Steve Jobs

You process hundreds of pieces of information every day. Meeting notes, article highlights, podcast takeaways, random ideas that hit you during a walk. Some of that information needs to be stored quickly and found later. Some of it needs to be understood, connected, and turned into something new. These are two completely different mental activities. Most people treat them as one, and that is exactly why their PKM system either becomes a graveyard of unprocessed notes or collapses under its own weight.

This lesson introduces two distinct thinking processes: Shallow Thinking and Deep Thinking. Not as a hierarchy where one is better than the other, but as two layers of cognition, each with its own purpose, tools, and place in your knowledge workflow.

## 04. What Is Context

### Section 1 of 10

Context is two concrete actions: Tracing (recording where information came from) and Integrating (connecting it to your projects, elements, and topics).

Tracing creates retrieval hooks. Record the source at capture time, even if it is just one line.
Integrating places information into buckets tied to your real work, not generic topic folders.
Topic-based filing fails because "Marketing" with 800 notes helps nobody find anything.
Context triggers a chain: Tracing + Integrating = Understanding = Thinking = Action.
"The more connections you can make between pieces of information, the more securely each piece is wired in your memory." (Peter Brown, Make It Stick)

If you have looked into PKM, you have come across the word "context" over and over. Every article mentions it. Every framework treats it as a given. But almost nobody defines it in a way you can actually use.

That confusion comes from vague definitions that sound intellectual but offer zero practical guidance. "In which context will I want to come across this idea again?" is the classic advice. The problem: when you capture information, you usually have no clue when or why you will want it again. The question is unanswerable at capture time. It creates decision paralysis, not clarity.

We need to cut through the fog. In How to perfectly store a note? from Digital Note-Taking like a Pro, you already experienced why source details and purpose matter when storing notes. That was context in action, even if we did not call it by name. Now we formalize it. For our purposes, context consists of two specific actions: TRACING and INTEGRATING. No philosophy. No abstract frameworks. Two concrete actions that transform how you store and retrieve information for the rest of your life.

## 05. What Is an Inbox?

### Section 1 of 11

An inbox is a temporary holding space for information you cannot route to its permanent home right now.

Temporary means items pass through. If notes sit in your inbox for a week, it is a junk drawer.
Processing is not doing. Route each item in seconds. Read, research, and deep work happen later.
Use two inboxes: one for Outer World content (articles, links) and one for Inner World thoughts (your ideas).
Clear your inbox daily in five minutes. Small batches stay manageable, and fresh items are easier to route.
"Wisdom is knowing what to do next; Skill is knowing how to do it, and Virtue is doing it." (David Starr Jordan)

You know where things should go. You just cannot always put them there right now. You are in a meeting, on a call, walking between appointments, or deep inside a problem that needs your full attention. Information arrives at the worst possible moment. You either interrupt what you are doing to file it properly, or you trust your memory to hold it until later. Both options are bad. Interruption kills focus. Memory drops things.

An inbox eliminates that choice entirely. It gives you a third option: capture now, route later. Simple as that. But the simplicity is deceptive, because most people use inboxes wrong, and wrong inbox habits create more problems than having no inbox at all.

If What Is Context? taught you how to make information findable, and The Capturing Beast taught you what deserves capturing in the first place, this lesson teaches you where captured items land before they reach their permanent home. The inbox is the bridge between "I just found something" and "I know exactly where this belongs."

## 06. What Is a Bucket?

### Section 1 of 12

A bucket is a permanent, contextualized storage area organized around your actual work and life, not generic topic folders.

Three bucket categories: Current Projects (active work), Key Elements (ongoing life areas), and Topics (three deep-dive subjects).
Inboxes are temporary waypoints. Buckets are permanent destinations where knowledge lives with context.
Cross-reference one item across multiple buckets. One insight, multiple access points.
Project buckets get archived when done. Topic buckets rotate quarterly. Key Element buckets grow forever.
"We do not learn from experience. We learn from reflecting on experience." John Dewey

You have captured dozens of notes this week. Meeting insights, article excerpts, project ideas, a voice memo from your commute. Your inbox processed them all. But now what? Where do these items go so you can actually find them six weeks from now when you need them for a client presentation or a strategic decision?

That question is the reason buckets exist. A bucket is a storage area that provides context when you store an idea. Its purpose is not to demand action. Its purpose is to facilitate action when the moment arrives. If you completed the lesson What Is an Inbox?, you learned that an inbox is a temporary, centralized collection point for items that need routing and processing. A bucket is the opposite: a permanent or semi-permanent home where knowledge lives, contextualized and ready, for as long as it remains relevant to your work and life.

## 07. Inner World and Outer World

### Section 1 of 10

Every piece of information comes from one of two sources: the Outer World (content others created) or the Inner World (your own thoughts and ideas).

Outer World: articles, books, podcasts, reports, social posts. High volume, needs heavy filtering.
Inner World: your reflections, ideas, connections, observations. Lower volume, almost always worth capturing.
Each world needs its own inbox and capture tool because they arrive differently and require different processing.
This distinction shapes your entire PKM architecture: which tools you pick, which workflows you follow, and how you route information.
"In the age of information, misinformation is the real enemy. It is essential to verify sources and seek out multiple perspectives to truly understand the world around us." (Barack Obama)

Every piece of information you encounter comes from one of two places. Either it comes from outside you, or it comes from inside you. This sounds simple. It is simple. And it changes everything about how you build your Personal Knowledge Management system.

In What is a note?, you first encountered this distinction between information from the outside world and your own original thinking. You saw it as a practical tool for understanding note types: external content you consume versus ideas you create. Now it is time to formalize that distinction into the framework that shapes your entire PKM architecture.

## 08. MY LIFE

### Section 1 of 9

MY LIFE is the master structure that organizes your entire PKM into three categories: Current Projects, Key Elements, and Topics.

Current Projects: active work with start and end dates. Archive the bucket when the project finishes.
Key Elements: ongoing life areas (company, team, health, family) that never have a deadline.
Topics: three subjects you are intentionally exploring this quarter. Rotate them when priorities shift.
If information does not fit any of the three categories, it does not belong in your system. That is the filter.

Let me ask you something.

Ever feel like you're drowning in information, and still missing the stuff that actually matters?

You're not alone.

After working with thousands of busy professionals, we've found that the first step to overcoming information overload is building a structure that mirrors how your brain naturally processes information.

That's exactly what the MY LIFE concept does.

Think of MY LIFE as the mental architecture of your PKM system.

## 09. GOALS — The Layer That Turns Structure Into Movement

### Section 1 of 8

Goals are the operating layer that turns MY LIFE from a static archive into a system that pulls your life forward.

A Goal is a destination, not a road. The test: if I reached it, would I know?
Every Goal in MY LIFE anchors to a Key Element. Never to a Project. Never to a Topic.
Two or three Goals per quarter is plenty. More dilutes them; fewer under-leverages the layer.
A Key Element with no Goal drifts back to archive. A Goal with no Key Element drifts to wish.
If you want to set a Goal on a Topic, that's a signal the Topic is asking to graduate, not permission to bend the rule.

There's a line from Lewis Carroll that lands the whole problem in one breath:

"If you don't know where you are going, any road will get you there."

You can build a perfect MY LIFE. Four buckets. All the right items. Every capture routed correctly.

And still be standing still.

## 10. The Promotion Path — When a Topic Graduates to a Key Element

### Section 1 of 8

When a Topic stops being exploration and starts carrying a defined outcome, it has graduated. Promote it to a Key Element so a Goal can anchor properly.

Topics rotate (quarterly). Key Elements persist (multi-year). Different timelines, different jobs.
The graduation signal is internal: "I want to commit to a measurable result on this."
French is the cleanest example. Topic until a fluency target shows up; then it earns Key Element status.
The promotion takes about two minutes: rename the bucket, move it to the Key Element list, anchor the Goal.
The reverse also applies. When something stops being permanent, archive it and close out its Goals.

A quick recap before we start.

The rule from the last lesson:

Every Goal in MY LIFE anchors to a Key Element. Never to a Project. Never to a Topic.

That rule sits cleanly until you run into a Topic that has, quietly, started carrying a real outcome. You know the feeling. The exploration is no longer just exploration. There's something specific you want to commit to. The Topic is showing signs of being more than a Topic.

That's where this lesson lives.

# Layer 2: Workflows

## 01. The Goal-Setting Workflow — Anchor, Decompose, Route

### Section 1 of 8

Three steps turn a Goal into something that actually gets done: name the destination, anchor it to a Key Element, decompose into Projects and Habits.

The constraint that makes this work: a Goal can only be achieved through a Project or a Habit. There is no third shape.
Step 1: write the achieved state in one sentence with a number. "Lose ten kilos by Q3." If you'd know you reached it, it's a destination.
Step 2: anchor to a Key Element. No anchor, no Goal. Either promote the Topic or release the wish.
Step 3: name the Project (one-time effort) and/or Habit (recurring rhythm) carrying the Goal. Never neither.
Quarterly rhythm: set, execute, review, rotate. Orphan Goals get decomposed or released; drifting execution gets reconnected or closed.

You've got the structure. Four buckets in MY LIFE.

You've got the operating layer. Goals anchored to Key Elements.

You've got the move that handles the edge case. Promote a Topic when it's carrying an outcome.

What's left is the workflow that turns all of that into something you actually run.

That's this lesson.

## 02. The Capturing Beast

### Section 1 of 9

The Capturing Beast is a three-layer filter that decides what information deserves a place in your system and what you should let go.

Layer 1: Does it relate to a Current Project? Layer 2: Does it impact a Key Element? Layer 3: Does it connect to one of your three Topics?
If it fails all three layers, let it go. No guilt, no "just in case" bookmarks.
Expect to capture 50-70% less than before. Less volume means more signal.
Replace FOMO with JOMO (Joy of Missing Out). Clarity about your priorities makes saying "no" feel like relief.

There's a quote from Shane Parrish that stays with me:

"The world has an unlimited number of problems for you to solve. If you don't solve the problem of which problems to solve, you'll be overwhelmed by them."

The same is true for information.

The world has an unlimited supply of it. If you don't solve the problem of which information deserves a place in your system, you'll be overwhelmed by all of it.

That's what this workflow does. It solves that problem.

It's called The Capturing Beast. It's the workflow that activates the structure you just learned in MY LIFE.

## 03. Workflow 1: DISCOVER

### Section 1 of 11

Discovery is Stage 1 of the Knowledge Management Cycle. It covers how information finds you and how you find information.

Two modes: intentional discovery (researching with purpose) and accidental discovery (stumbling onto something useful).
Intentional discovery is focused and efficient. Accidental discovery needs The Capturing Beast as a shield.
Uncontrolled browsing burns time on content that connects to nothing. Recognize the pattern and stop.
Your only job at this stage: notice information. Capture and processing happen in later stages.
"Knowledge wants to talk. Wisdom wants to listen." Haemin Sunim

You are sitting in an airport lounge, scrolling through your phone between flights. A headline catches your eye: "How AI is Reshaping Supply Chain Management." You tap it. Twenty minutes later, you have read three articles, saved two bookmarks, and opened four browser tabs. None of this connects to any project you are working on. None of it relates to your quarterly goals. You just consumed twenty minutes of information you did not need, and you did not even notice it happening.

## 04. Workflow 2: CAPTURE

### Section 1 of 12

Capture is the bridge between noticing something useful and having it safely inside your system, done in under five seconds.

Use two inboxes: Outer World (articles, links, reports) and Inner World (your own ideas, reflections, observations).
Capture the item, then add minimal context: source, why it matters, one relevant bucket.
Speed is everything. If your capture tool takes more than five seconds, you will skip it under pressure.
Capture is not processing. Save now, evaluate later during your daily inbox clearing.
"The palest ink is better than the best memory." Chinese Proverb

You are in the middle of a project review when your manager mentions a competitor's new pricing model. You know it matters. You make a mental note to look into it later. Three hours pass. By the time you sit down at your desk, the detail is gone. You remember the conversation happened. You remember it felt important. But the specifics, the exact number, the product name, the comparison your manager made, all of it has evaporated.

## 05. Workflow 3: PROCESS

### Section 1 of 11

Process is where you decide what deserves your deeper investment using The Thought Lab, a four-question evaluation framework.

Ask: Does this improve my knowledge? Is it worth exploring? Does it impact my projects? Are there clear next steps?
YES to any question moves the item to Action. NO to all keeps it in your Shallow Thinking system.
Wait a day or two before processing. Fresh eyes separate real value from initial excitement.
Most captured items do not need deep attention. That is the system working correctly, not a failure.
"The art of being wise is the art of knowing what to overlook." William James

You saved an article last Tuesday. It felt important at the time. Something about a new framework for managing remote teams. You tagged it, dropped it in your inbox, and moved on. Four days later you open your inbox to process it. The excitement is gone. The headline no longer feels urgent. With fresh eyes, you realize the article only repeats what you already know. So you archive it and move to the next item. That decision took ten seconds. It saved you thirty minutes of reading and connecting something that had nothing new to offer.

## 06. Workflow 4: ACTION

### Section 1 of 10

Action has two paths: route clear tasks to your task/project system, or send complex ideas to your Deep Thinking environment for further exploration.

Path 1: You know what to do. Write a concrete next step and move it to PPM (personal) or BPM (team).
Path 2: You need more thinking. Move the information to your visual canvas or Deep Thinking workspace.
This is where PKM connects to your task management system. Knowledge stays in PKM, actions move out.
Information that never becomes a decision, task, or deliverable is just well-organized storage.
"Vision without execution is hallucination." Thomas Edison

You have been filtering, analyzing, and deciding for three full stages. DISCOVER brought information into your world. CAPTURE preserved it before it disappeared. PROCESS evaluated it through The Thought Lab and gave you a clear YES: this information deserves your investment. Now you stand at the most consequential moment in the entire Knowledge Management Cycle. The moment where knowledge either becomes movement or stays as notes in a system nobody revisits.

## 07. Workflow 5: SHARE

### Section 1 of 10

Share is the final stage where personal knowledge becomes collective knowledge, moving from your PKM into BKM or public output.

Not everything should be shared. Most processed knowledge stays private. Share only what genuinely benefits others.
Sharing stress-tests your ideas against other perspectives and reveals blind spots you cannot see alone.
This is where PKM meets BKM. Personal insights become team documentation, proposals, or published content.
Sharing completes the cycle and restarts it: feedback from others creates new information to discover.
"Sharing knowledge is the most fundamental act of friendship. Because it is a way you can give something without losing something." Richard Stallman

You have been through four stages. You discovered information worth your attention. You captured it into your system. You processed it through deep evaluation and connection-building. You turned it into concrete actions tied to your goals. Now comes the question that separates a personal knowledge hoard from a living knowledge engine: is any of this worth putting back into the world?

Not everything is. Most of what you process will stay in your personal system, serving you quietly. That is fine. That is the design working correctly. But some insights and patterns you uncover will be genuinely valuable to others. When that happens, the SHARE workflow gives you a disciplined process for getting those ideas into the hands of people who can use them.
